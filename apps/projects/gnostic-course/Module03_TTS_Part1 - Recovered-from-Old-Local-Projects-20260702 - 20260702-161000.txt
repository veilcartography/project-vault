Lesson One: The Nag Hammadi Discovery.

In December 1945, a young Egyptian farmer named Muhammad Ali al-Samman was digging for fertiliser near the cliffs of Jabal al-Tarif, close to the town of Nag Hammadi in Upper Egypt. His mattock struck a large sealed clay jar. Fearing it might contain a djinn, he hesitated. Then, hoping it contained gold, he broke it open.

Inside were thirteen leather-bound papyrus codices — ancient books — wrapped in leather and sealed for preservation. They had been hidden there in the fourth century of the Common Era, most likely by monks from the nearby Pachomian monastery, concealing them from the Church's campaign to destroy heretical texts. They had lain undisturbed for one thousand seven hundred years.

The Nag Hammadi Library contains fifty-two texts in Coptic, translated from Greek originals. They include: the Gospel of Thomas — one hundred and fourteen sayings attributed to Jesus, with no narrative, no miracles, no resurrection story, pure teaching. The Gospel of Philip — a Valentinian text on the sacraments and the nature of the divine feminine. The Apocryphon of John — the secret teaching of John, containing the full Gnostic cosmology. The Gospel of Truth — a meditation on the nature of error and the return to the Father. Thunder, Perfect Mind — a remarkable poem in the voice of the divine feminine. And the Hypostasis of the Archons — a detailed account of the Archons and their role in the material world.

How to approach these texts: read slowly. Sit with one passage in silence. Let it work on you. The meaning often arrives not during reading but in the quiet afterwards.

Lesson Two: Gospel of Thomas Deep Dive.

The Gospel of Thomas opens with a single sentence that sets the entire tone: These are the secret sayings that the living Jesus spoke and Didymos Judas Thomas recorded. And he said: Whoever discovers the interpretation of these sayings will not taste death.

This is not a promise of physical immortality. It is a statement about gnosis: the one who truly understands these sayings — not intellectually, but experientially — has found something that transcends the fear of death.

Three sayings for deep study.

Saying Three: If those who lead you say to you, See, the Kingdom is in heaven, then the birds of heaven will precede you. But the Kingdom is within you and it is outside you. When you know yourselves, then you will be known. The Kingdom is not a future reward in another place. It is a present reality, accessible through self-knowledge.

Saying Twenty-Two: When you make the two into one, and when you make the inner like the outer and the outer like the inner, and the upper like the lower, and when you make male and female into a single one — then you will enter the Kingdom. This saying is about the integration of opposites — the inner work of making whole what has been divided.

Saying Seventy: If you bring forth what is within you, what you have will save you. If you do not have that within you, what you do not have within you will kill you. The divine spark within you is your salvation. Suppressing it is the source of all suffering.

The reading method: choose one saying. Read it once. Put the text down. Sit in silence for five to ten minutes. Do not analyse. Simply hold the saying in awareness and notice what arises. Write what comes. Return the next day and repeat.
