Lesson One: Nous: The Eye of the Heart.

Nous — pronounced noos — is the ancient Greek word for the faculty of direct spiritual perception. It is not the rational mind — the part that analyses, categorises, and argues. Nous is the capacity to see truth directly, without the mediation of thought.

The Gnostics called it the eye of the heart. Where the rational mind processes information, nous perceives reality. Where the intellect constructs understanding, nous recognises what is already known at a deeper level.

In the Gnostic framework, nous is the faculty through which gnosis is received. You cannot think your way to gnosis. You can only become still enough for nous to function — and then recognise what it shows you.

The practice of meditation is the practice of quieting those patterns long enough for nous to function.

Lesson Two: Gnostic Meditation Practice and Contemplative Self-Inquiry.

Two core techniques form the foundation of Gnostic meditation practice.

Technique One: Breath and Stillness. Sit comfortably with your spine upright. Close your eyes. Bring your attention to the natural rhythm of your breath — not controlling it, simply observing it. When the mind wanders, gently return attention to the breath without judgement. The goal is not to stop thinking. The goal is to create a gap between thoughts — a space of stillness in which nous can operate. Begin with ten minutes daily. The Gnostic tradition recommends the same time each day — dawn is traditional, as the liminal quality of early morning supports inner perception.

Technique Two: Light Visualisation — the Luminous Body. After establishing stillness through breath, bring your attention to the centre of your chest. Imagine a small point of warm golden light there — the divine spark, the pneuma. With each breath, allow that light to expand gently outward, filling your body, then extending beyond it. Do not force this. If nothing visual comes, simply hold the intention of the light. The practice is the intention, not the image.

Both techniques can be combined in a single twenty-minute session: ten minutes of breath and stillness, followed by ten minutes of light visualisation. This is the recommended daily practice for the remainder of this course.

Contemplation and Self-Inquiry. Contemplation differs from meditation in that it involves a question held in awareness rather than simple stillness. The core question: Who am I beyond this body? This is not a philosophical question to be answered with words. It is a question to be held in awareness during meditation — a direction of attention rather than a request for information.

Begin to notice the inner patterns that arise during meditation: fear, the sense that something is wrong. Distraction, the mind's preference for almost anything over stillness. And ego, the subtle voice that evaluates and judges the meditation itself. These are not enemies to be fought. They are patterns to be recognised. Recognition — simply seeing them clearly, without engagement — is the beginning of liberation from them.
